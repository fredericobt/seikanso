A good tool disappears. You stop thinking about it and start thinking with it.

This is the standard I hold all software to. Does it get out of the way? Does it amplify what I'm trying to do without demanding attention for itself?

## The Problem with Feature Lists

Most tools compete on features. They add, never subtract. Every update brings more surface area, more menus, more settings to configure.

The tools I've kept longest are the ones that said no more than they said yes.

## Criteria

When evaluating a tool:

1. Can you use it without reading documentation?
2. Does it make your output better, or just different?
3. Will you still want it in two years?

If the answer to all three is yes, keep it.
